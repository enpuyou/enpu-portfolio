title: Running a record store in Suzhou
date: Jun 2025
description: Eight months managing a vinyl shop and performance venue in China. What I learned about music, community, and logistics.
---

The store was called *dv*. Small, two floors, a bar in the back. The owner had been building it for three years before I arrived, collecting records from Hong Kong dealers and setting up a system where the listening bar faced a small stage. The idea was that you could hear something live, then go buy the record. It worked, more or less.

I was between things — between jobs, between countries, thinking about what came next. I'd moved to Suzhou partly because a friend lived there and partly because I wanted to live somewhere that required me to pay attention.

## What the job actually was

On paper: inventory management, vendor relations, event logistics. In practice: being the person who knew where the shipping forms were, who to call when an artist's visa fell through, how to price a Coltrane reissue when you don't have Discogs comps in RMB.

Forty-plus events over eight months. Most of them small — thirty, forty people. Some of them larger. We booked artists from Japan, South Korea, Southeast Asia, occasionally Europe. The booking process was part email chain, part WeChat negotiation, part guesswork about what a Suzhou audience would show up for on a Tuesday.

## What I learned about inventory

Vinyl is physical. This sounds obvious but it changes how you think about it. Digital inventory is about information — SKUs, counts, locations. Physical inventory is about space, humidity, the specific weight of a box you have to move. We stored records in conditions that would horrify any serious collector. They mostly survived.

The supply chain is stranger than I expected. Some of our best stock came from a dealer in Guangzhou who would text a photo of a crate with no context and expect a decision by evening. Some came from an elderly man in Suzhou who brought in his own collection in paper bags. You learned to evaluate quickly and ask questions later.

> Running a small store teaches you that margin is not profit. Profit is what's left after you've paid for everything you forgot to account for.

## The revenue increase

The 30% figure I put on my resume is real, and I'm genuinely uncertain how much credit I can take for it. We expanded the events calendar, tightened the booking process, added a few retail partnerships. But a lot of it was just Suzhou getting more comfortable with the kind of thing we were doing — younger audiences, more interest in analog sound, a general feeling that the shop had been there long enough to be trusted.

That might be the most honest version of "contributed to revenue growth" I've ever encountered.

## What I brought back

A habit of buying records in unfamiliar contexts. A better sense of how communities form around specific physical spaces. An understanding that logistics is fundamentally about attention — noticing what's missing before it becomes a problem.

And about forty records I had to ship back in pieces because I couldn't fit them in my bags.
